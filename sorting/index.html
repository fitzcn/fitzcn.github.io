Sorting out sorting

An introduction to computational thinking using sorting algorithms

Overview

The concept of algorithms and their design can be difficult to grasp in the abstract. The purpose of this lesson is to first build out studentsâ€™ understanding of how algorithms are implemented through activities and pseudocode, then apply those ideas in an exploration of commonly known sorting algorithms.

Order of activities

The lesson is broken into four different sections, each with a distinct goal.

  1. Algorithms role and function
  2. Sorting Activities
  3. Pseudocode/Implementation
  4. Ways to evaluate

Student Outcomes

Students will be able to:

  1. Identify problem spaces for algorithmic intervention
  2. Generate pseudocode for algorithm design
  3. Make implementation decisions given the problem space
  4. Evaluate algorithms

Algorithms' Role and Function

We encounter algorithms constantly in our daily routines. In this section of the workshop, we begin our discussion on the roles of algorithms in shaping our experiences.

What do algorithms do for you?

Working with a partner, work on the following questions/prompts.

  • Find an example of when you think you encountered an algorithm yesterday.
  • Describe the context and setting. What were you trying to do?
  • What did this algorithm do for you?
  • What alternatives did you have?
  • What steps did the algorithm take to come up with its response to you?
  • What decisions or assumptions were made in the algorithms' design?

A working definition of algorithm

Given the discussion from the previous task, work independently to write down a working definition of an algorithm. Once done, share your definition with your partner. When the partners are done sharing, we will come together as a class to create a single working definition.

Given this our new working definition, what do we think of the following:

  • What can be considered an algorithm?
  • What is not an algorithm?
  • Where else do we encounter algorithms in our lives?
  • What are examples of non-digital algorithms?
  • What considerations are important in the development of algorithms?

Sorting without Code

Now that we have a working sense of what an algorithm is and what it does, we are going to work through three activities that require us to make our own algorithms. The goal for each of the activities is to create a sorted line of everyone based on some type of information. Sorting algorithms are good for working through understanding algorithms given that they can provide a range of complexity and demostrate lots of considerations of algorithm design in a tangible manner.

By name

On one of the index cards that you have, write down your name. Stand up and bring your card to the front of the classroom; if other students are already there, simply stand before or after them where there is room to form a line. Once everyone is line, sort yourselves while making mental notes about the steps you take to do so.

Once everyone is sorted, you will be asked to return to your seats. Working with a partner, record the steps to the Name Sort algorithm you made as a class.

By birthday

On the second index card, write down your birthday. As before, afterwards stand up and take the card with you to the front of the room; if other students are already there, stand before or after them where there is room. Once everyone is in line, sort yourselves again. You may not use the same steps you used for the Name Sort.

Once everyone is sorted, return to your seats. Again, working with a partner, record the steps to Birthday Sort.

By color of shirt

On the third index card, write down the dominant color of your shirt (white, red, orange, yellow, green, blue, purple, brown, black). For a final time, stand up and take the card with you to the front of the room; if other students are already there, stand before or after them where there is room. Once everyone is in line, sort yourselves by color. You may not use the same steps you used for the Name Sort or Birthday Sort.

Once everyone is sorted, return to your seats. Again, working with a partner, record the steps to Color Sort.

Sorting with Code

Now that we have three examples of pseudo code, we are going to work together to implement some of them in Python. While there are many different ways to sort sets of data, included are four standard options below. Some of our sorting algorithms may map closely to these. In any case, looking across ours and those included below will help shape our understanding of algorithm design.

Bubble sort

Bubble sort is one of the most simple sorting algorithms. It starts at the beginning of the data set and compares the first two items, sorting them. It then works through the data continuing to sort in comparisons of two items. Once it has worked through the list the first time, it will have sorted the highest item to its last place. The algorithm them starts again and works through the set, repeating this process until no swaps are made in a final pass.

Insertion sort

Insertion sort works a little differently than bubble sort, as it requires fewer passes across the items. It takes the first item and puts it into a new set. Then it works across the first set, item by item, and places it in the new set in its proper location. This method comes at the cost of being inefficient on larger data sets and sets that are not relatively sorted to begin with.

Merge sort

Merge sort works on the premise that sorting subsets that are already sorted is effecient. It works by sorting pairs of items and them merging the sorted pairs together. It continues merging sorted sets until it merges the whole set together.

Quick sort

Of the sorting algorithms presented today, Quick Sort is the most complex. Using a pivot function, it breaks the original set up by a value and splits the items that are higher or lower than that value. It then follows the same procedure recursively through the higher and lower subsets (and subsets of subsets, etc) until the whole set is sorted.
